## Issue Description

### Problem 1: Naming Collision with Random Pod Names
During our evaluation of Kueue, we encountered an issue related to the naming of pods when creating them from a Job specification. Kueue appends random characters to the end of pod names, and this approach occasionally leads to naming collisions among pods when job names are identical.

### Problem 2: Unexpected Behavior When Cluster Runs Out of Resources
If the previous issue is solved manually (creating jobs with unique names manually), when the cluster runs out of resources to create new pods, Kueue doesn't appear to get stuck. Instead, it deletes a subset of pods that have already finished their job and then continues scheduling. This behavior is not as expected.

### Expected Behavior
1. Pods generated by Kueue should have a lower risk of naming collisions, especially when job names are the same.
2. Kueue should handle resource exhaustion gracefully without deleting pods that have completed their tasks. Deletion of finished pods can lead to data loss and unexpected behavior.

### Context
The issues were observed during a test where the cluster ran out of resources for creating new pods. However, instead of getting stuck, Kueue deleted a subset of pods that had already completed their jobs, and then it continued scheduling new pods. This behavior was inconsistent with expectations and could potentially lead to data loss or other issues in production environments.

## Steps to Reproduce (for Both Problems)

1. Deploy Kueue using the provided configuration.
2. Create a large number of jobs (2000), some with identical names (for Problem 1) and utilize significant cluster resources (for Problem 2).
3. Observe pod naming and Kueue's behavior when the cluster exhausts resources.

### Comment by [@trasc](https://github.com/trasc) — 2023-10-10T14:54:52Z

> Kueue should handle resource exhaustion gracefully without deleting pods that have completed their tasks. Deletion of finished pods can lead to data loss and unexpected behavior.

Kueue is mainly concerned to manege the admission of [Workloads](https://kueue.sigs.k8s.io/docs/concepts/workload/) within a [cluster queue](https://kueue.sigs.k8s.io/docs/concepts/cluster_queue/) [resource quotas](https://kueue.sigs.k8s.io/docs/concepts/cluster_queue/#resource-groups) defined. It is not in the scope of Kueue to check the status of the resources within the cluster. Check [the overview page](https://kueue.sigs.k8s.io/docs/overview/) for more details.

For clarity, kueue doesn't delete (actually, it is suspend) finished pods, excluding when higher priority jobs preempt lower priority Jobs.
So whether finished jobs are removed depends on batch/job features such as ttlSecondsAfterFinished.

### Comment by [@alculquicondor](https://github.com/alculquicondor) — 2023-10-10T15:43:02Z

> Kueue should handle resource exhaustion gracefully without deleting pods that have completed their tasks. Deletion of finished pods can lead to data loss and unexpected behavior.

Are you saying that the Pods are marked with status either Succeeded or Failed?
This is also not a behavior of Kueue, but Kubernetes. Once a Pod is terminal, it can be removed by the garbage collector to free space in the database.

This doesn't cause problems for the tracking of the completion of the job (as long as you are using kubernetes 1.25 or newer). What kind of data lose are you experiencing?

If you have any other processes that depend on the pods to continue to exist, then you can use finalizers to keep the pods while you need them. https://kubernetes.io/docs/concepts/overview/working-with-objects/finalizers/
