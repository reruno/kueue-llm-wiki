#### What this PR does / why we need it:

During elastic scale-up, the controller may mark the existing WorkloadSlice as finished before the replacement slice is successfully admitted. If the admission of the new slice fails, for example due to transient API or scheduling errors, the Job can remain active and unsuspended while no slice is admitted and capacity accounting is not restored. This results in an inconsistent state where the Job appears to be running despite having no admitted WorkloadSlice, which should instead trigger suspension and quota restoration.

### Comment by [@ichekrygin](https://github.com/ichekrygin) — 2026-02-17T01:12:09Z

> Just brainstorm ideas, is there anyway to introduce a "transa...

This is effectively what we are doing here, but in reverse. I summarized the available options in https://github.com/kubernetes-sigs/kueue/issues/9015#issuecomment-3861776434, and it would be great to continue the discussion there.

The key distinction is that in Kueue the `Finished` condition is both terminal and irreversible. Once a WorkloadSlice reaches `Finished`, it is permanently considered complete and there is no mechanism to revert it back to an admitted or reserving state.

During elastic scale-up, the transition consists of two logically coupled steps:

1. Mark the old slice as `Finished`
2. Admit the replacement slice

These steps are not atomic. If step (1) succeeds but step (2) fails, even temporarily, the system enters a state where:

* the old slice is permanently finished,
* the new slice is not admitted,
* and there is no admitted slice holding quota on behalf of the Job.

At that point, we cannot roll back to the previous state, because `Finished` is irreversible. The only viable recovery path is to suspend or otherwise unwind the Job so that quota accounting and admission semantics remain consistent.

While admission is often guaranteed to eventually succeed under StrictFIFO or sticky workloads, this assumption does not hold universally. Admission may be delayed or prevented due to quota changes, admission checks, API failures, or controller restarts. The important part is that once we commit the irreversible transition, we must ensure the system remains consistent even if the second step does not immediately complete.

I outlined alternative approaches and their respective trade-offs in the linked comment. PTAL, @hiboyang.

### Comment by [@ichekrygin](https://github.com/ichekrygin) — 2026-02-17T01:16:40Z

> Actually, before I dive deep into this code. Let me re-check if this issue is only in case something would make the "new slice" not admitable permanently, eg changing CQ quotas, or maybe admission checks?

You’re right that under StrictFIFO and the sticky workload mechanism, the replacement slice is typically guaranteed to be admitted eventually, assuming quota and admission conditions remain stable.

However, the main concern here is not permanent admission failure, but the intermediate quota gap created by marking the old slice as `Finished` before the replacement slice is admitted. During that window, the Job remains active but no WorkloadSlice holds quota on its behalf, which breaks the usual invariant that active workloads have an admitted slice accounting for their usage.

This can occur even if admission eventually succeeds, for example due to transient admission delays, controller restarts, or temporary admission failures. The issue is therefore about maintaining consistent quota accounting and lifecycle semantics during the transition, not just eventual admission.

There is a broader design discussion in https://github.com/kubernetes-sigs/kueue/issues/9015 around different ways to handle this, including alternatives such as quota bump (temporarily reserving quota for both slices) versus allowing a quota gap and relying on recovery mechanisms.

This PR takes the conservative approach of restoring consistency when a quota gap is detected by suspending the Job, rather than leaving it active without an admitted slice. This ensures correct accounting regardless of the admission timing or outcome, while leaving room for future improvements to make the transition atomic or eliminate the quota gap entirely.

  If the new slice is not admitted within the 1 second grace period, the slice will be considered "runaway", and the Job will be suspended as a safety measure.
  
  The 1 second interval is not a requirement for admission, but rather a grace period that allows the scheduler time to complete the admission of the replacement slice under normal conditions. If admission does not occur within that window, the controller assumes the transition did not complete successfully and triggers recovery by suspending the Job.
  
  Under normal conditions, marking the old slice as `Finished` and admitting the replacement slice are sequential steps that occur within the same scheduling cycle, so there is no observable gap between them.
  
  However, under abnormal conditions, it is possible for the `Finished` step to succeed while the admission of the replacement slice fails, for example due to transient API errors, scheduler restarts, or ClusterQueue state changes. In such cases, the scheduler is expected to retry admission. The 1 second grace period provides a window for the scheduler to re-process and complete the failed admission as part of a subsequent scheduling cycle.

  There are a few important considerations here:
  
  First, this mechanism is specifically addressing an edge case involving a transient failure during the scheduling cycle, combined with the replacement slice becoming immediately ineligible for admission, for example due to quota changes or competing admissions. Under normal conditions, marking the old slice as `Finished` and admitting the new slice happen within the same scheduling cycle, so the grace period is never exercised. The 1 second interval is therefore not part of the steady-state path, but rather a safeguard for an abnormal transition scenario.
  
  Second, allowing an extended quota gap is itself a sub-optimal outcome. As discussed in [the updated issue](https://github.com/kubernetes-sigs/kueue/issues/9015#issuecomment-3912298762), the available options involve trade-offs between quota gap, quota bump, and recovery semantics. This PR intentionally takes the conservative approach of bounding the quota gap and restoring consistency if admission does not recover quickly.
  
  Finally, since WorkloadSlice-based elasticity is currently an alpha feature, I would suggest starting with a simple hardcoded value and gaining operational experience before introducing additional configuration surface. If we observe real-world scenarios where the grace period proves insufficient, we can revisit this and make it configurable with clearer guidance on appropriate tuning.
